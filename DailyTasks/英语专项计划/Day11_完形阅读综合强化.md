**Screen Time and Health**

Do you spend a lot of time looking at screens? Many teenagers today use smartphones, tablets, and computers for several hours every day. While technology brings us many benefits, too much screen time can be harmful to our health.

First, spending too much time on screens is bad for our eyes. Staring at screens for a long time can cause eye strain, dryness, and headaches. Doctors recommend the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds.

Second, screen time before bedtime can affect our sleep. The blue light from screens tricks our brain into thinking it's still daytime. This makes it harder to fall asleep. Experts suggest not using screens at least one hour before going to bed.

Third, too much screen time means less physical activity. When we're glued to our screens, we're not moving our bodies. This can lead to health problems like obesity and weak muscles.

However, screens are not all bad. They help us learn, communicate with friends and family, and enjoy entertainment. The key is to use them wisely. Set time limits, take regular breaks, and balance screen time with other activities like reading books, playing sports, or spending time outdoors.

**The Story of Malala**

Malala Yousafzai was born in Pakistan in 1997. She grew up in a beautiful valley where her father ran a school. From a young age, Malala loved learning and going to school.

However, when Malala was 10 years old, the Taliban took control of her region. They said girls should not go to school. Many schools for girls were closed. Malala was very upset. She believed that every girl had the right to education.

Malala started to speak out. She wrote a blog for the BBC using a false name, telling the world about what was happening. She gave interviews and spoke at events. She became famous for standing up for girls' education.

In 2012, when Malala was 15, a Taliban gunman shot her on her way home from school. She was seriously injured and had to go to England for treatment. Amazingly, she survived.

After recovering, Malala didn't stop. She continued to fight for girls' education. In 2014, at the age of 17, she became the youngest person ever to win the Nobel Peace Prize. Today, she continues her work through the Malala Fund, which helps girls around the world get an education.

Malala's story teaches us that one person, even a young person, can make a big difference in the world.

**The Future of Food: Insects?**

What will we eat in the future? Scientists say that insects might become an important part of our diet. While eating insects might sound strange to many people, about 2 billion people around the world already eat them regularly.

Why insects? First, insects are very nutritious. They contain a lot of protein, vitamins, and minerals. Some insects have even more protein than beef or chicken. Second, raising insects is much better for the environment than raising animals like cows or pigs. Insects need less food, water, and space. They also produce fewer greenhouse gases.

There are more than 1,900 types of edible insects. Some of the most popular are crickets, grasshoppers, and mealworms. In Thailand, you can buy fried insects at street markets. In Mexico, people eat tacos filled with grasshoppers. Some restaurants in Europe and North America are starting to serve insect dishes.

Of course, many people still feel uncomfortable about eating insects. Food companies are trying to make insect products more acceptable. For example, they grind insects into powder and use it to make protein bars, pasta, and bread. This way, people can get the benefits of insect protein without actually seeing the insects.

Will insects replace traditional meat? Probably not completely. But they could become an important alternative, especially as the world's population continues to grow and we need to find more sustainable ways to feed everyone.
